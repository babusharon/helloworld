How I Started Observing the Psychology of Women 👩

To explain this, I need to go back to my childhood—the summer vacations. During those days, I used to visit my mother’s home. For me, it was a chance to meet my cousins, while for my mom, it was about visiting her parents.
I always looked forward to seeing my brothers there. They had this way of breaking through my silence and shyness shortly after we met. We played all kinds of games together—indoor games like carrom (not chess!), and outdoor sports like cricket.
The result? I lost to my brothers in carrom—they played it regularly. But in cricket, even though I played often at school, I struggled at first. Still, they were surprised by some of my cricket shots, even when they bowled at medium pace.
After the games, we often shifted to more serious discussions—mostly about studies. By then, our minds were calm, composed, and sharpened from all the play. (I won’t reveal my cousins’ real names, so I’ll call them B1, B2, and B3 according to their age—B3 being the youngest, then me.)
B1: “Hey Shar, who’s the topper in your class?”
Me: “I am.” (Class had only 6 boys and 3 girls.)
B2: “Then what about the girls? What are they doing there?” (B1 and B3 laughed.)
Me: “One girl is second in the class.”
B3: “How is she? What’s her name?” (smiles)
Me: “Teena.” (Name changed for privacy)
B1: “Is she beautiful?”
Me: (smiles shyly) “Yes.”
B1: “What’s her full name?”
Me: “Teena Thampi.”
B2: “Christian?”
Me: “Yes.”
B1: “Is she really beautiful?”
At this point, I understood what was happening—they were teasing me, trying to see if I had a crush on her.
Me: “Ha! She sits on the boys’ bench near me and my friend. During a festival at the temple, Mom bought some bangles and asked me to give them to her when school reopened.”
I still remember the happiness on my brothers’ faces after I said that. There was no mockery—just genuine joy. Even B3, the youngest, responded so wisely. That moment made me realize how relationships and expressions—especially from girls—have depth. And from there, I started observing people more carefully, especially girls, not to judge, but to understand.
